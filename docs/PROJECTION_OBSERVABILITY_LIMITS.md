# PROJECTION OBSERVABILITY LIMITS

## Core Problem

The recent benchmark series suggests a fundamental limitation:

```text
observable similarity
!=
structural equivalence

A system may appear structurally equivalent under a given projection while originating from fundamentally different generators.

The core issue is therefore not merely similarity measurement.

The deeper issue is:

projection observability


---

Projection vs Structure

Let:

S = latent structure
P(S) = observable projection

The observer never accesses the full structure directly.

The observer accesses only:

P(S)

which is a compressed observable surface.

This creates an important asymmetry:

different structures
can share
similar projections

Formally:

P(S1) ≈ P(S2)
does not imply
S1 ≈ S2

This is the central projection problem.


---

Observable vs Latent Structure

The experiments increasingly separate two domains:

Latent Structure

full generating organization

including:

hidden dependencies

causal organization

internal topology

generation dynamics

unobserved dimensions


Observable Structure

the measurable projection surface

including:

transition patterns

symbol frequencies

motif recurrence

graph topology

compressed signatures


The detector operates only on observable structure.

It does not access latent structure directly.


---

Projection Capacity

Every projection has finite capacity.

Meaning:

a projection cannot preserve infinite distinctions

Projection capacity determines:

how many structural differences
remain observable

Low-capacity projections compress aggressively.

High-capacity projections preserve more separation.

This creates the central tradeoff:

high observability
→ strong separation
→ low tolerance

low observability
→ high compression
→ false merges


---

Indistinguishability

Two systems become indistinguishable when:

their observable projections converge

even if their latent generators differ.

Formally:

P(S1) = P(S2)
while
S1 != S2

This creates:

projection indistinguishability

The benchmark false-merge traps are examples of this phenomenon.


---

Structural Collapse

Structural collapse occurs when projection compression removes enough information that structural recovery becomes unstable or impossible.

Sequence:

latent structure
→ projection
→ compression
→ ambiguity
→ indistinguishability
→ collapse

Collapse does not necessarily mean the structures became equal.

It means:

the observer can no longer reliably separate them


---

Failure Geometry

The experiments reveal multiple failure regions.

False Split Region

same structure
→ projection perturbation
→ artificial separation

False Merge Region

different structure
→ projection compression
→ artificial equivalence

Drift Region

partial deformation
→ unstable observability

Boundary Region

projection capacity exhausted

These regions form a geometry of observability failure.


---

Irreversibility

Some projection losses appear irreversible.

Meaning:

multiple latent states
map into the same projection

Once compressed:

recovery may become impossible

This implies:

perfect reconstruction
cannot be guaranteed
under bounded projection capacity

observability analysis
